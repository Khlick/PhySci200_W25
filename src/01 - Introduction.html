Statistical Significance

The alpha level of 0.05, a standard threshold for statistical significance, was popularized by Ronald A. Fisher in the 1920s. Originally suggested as a convenient cut-off for rejecting the null hypothesis, it implied a result would only occur by chance about 1 in 20 times under the null hypothesis.

Despite its arbitrary nature, the 0.05 level became widely adopted due to its balance between Type I and Type II errors. Over time, it has become the conventional standard across various scientific disciplines.

Recent debates challenge this standard, suggesting adjustments or alternative approaches like confidence intervals, effects sizes, or Bayesian statistics to better reflect the complexity of scientific evidence.

It is convenient to take this point as a limit in judging whether a deviation is to be considered significant or not.

-Ronald Fisher

Formal Hypothesis Testing

Hypothesis testing is very powerful, as it helps shed light on the likelihood that an observed effect could be due to random chance.

However, statistical significance is not foolproof, and it is possible to make the wrong decision, rejecting a true null hypothesis or not rejecting a false null hypothesis.

Hypothesis testing itself isn't the real problem, we use maths to model literally everything, but only in statistics do we find fallacy of prescribing a single number to "truth".

If you're not aware, which I suspect you are since no-one here is a mathematician or physicist, then all mathematical models are wrong. They are the distillation of how we think the world works, but they are not the world itself.

For example, in Newtonian mechanics, you may plug-in a "negative mass" and get a solution where the object accelerates into an applied force, which is clearly wrong. But it turns out you may observe something that looks like this behavior, but it's not actually a negative mass, there's something else going on.

This is why we need to be careful with our models, and why we need to be careful with our statistics.

IS THE SITUATION HOPELESS?

The Future Is Now!

Efron and Tibshirani point out that most of our familiar statistical approaches were originally developed in the 19th century, and that the computer age has allowed us to do things that were previously impossible.

General Rule: Use Simulations!

  • Calculate probabilities (p-values), estimate uncertainty, and test hypotheses by simulation, not by formulas based on dubious assumptions.

Steps:

  1. Construct/Formulate a chance model that represents the hypothesis
  2. Iterate the chance model thousands of times
  3. Inspect the results, i.e., count the number of times the event of interest occurs

This is an oversimplification, there are many nuances we'll cover later.

What's The Probability

It happens by chance?

How often will a mouse litter of 5 pups have at least 4 females?

The only assumptions we need to simulate the problem are: (A) the probability of any one pup being female is $\frac{1}{2}$ and (B) we only care about litters with 5 pups.

PAUSE: Anyone here know what analytical approach would be taught in a normal stats class?

How many possible outcomes are there? How many different "events" are there?

What is the probability of a pup being female?

Note: the assumptions for simulation are flexible.

Let's look at the analytical approach first.

Analytical (Formula) Approach

  1. The number of pups is 5 ($n = 5$, fixed) and the number of females is at least 4 ($x \geq 4$)
  2. Assume a "Null" hypothesis: The probability of a pup being female is always $\frac{1}{2}$ ($p_{\text{null}} = 0.5$)
  3. Assume each pup's gender is independent of the others
  4. Assume a binomial distribution: $X \sim \text{Binomial}(n, x, p_{\text{null}}) = p(x|n, p_{\text{null}}) = \frac{n!}{x!(n-x)!} p_{\text{null}}^x (1-p_{\text{null}})^{n-x}$
  5. Calculate the probability of the event of interest:
    $P(X \geq 4) = \sum_{x=4}^{5} \binom{5}{x} p_{\text{null}}^x (1-p_{\text{null}})^{5-x}$
$$\begin{align*} P(X \geq 4) &= \binom{5}{4}(0.5)^4(0.5)^1 + \binom{5}{5}(0.5)^5(0.5)^0 \\ &= \frac{5!}{4!(5-4)!}(0.5)^4(0.5)^1 + \frac{5!}{5!(5-5)!}(0.5)^5(0.5)^0 \\ &= 0.1875 \\ \therefore & \text{The probability of 4 or more girls in 5 pups is:} \\ p &= 0.1875 \end{align*}$$

Point out here that the p-value is the sum of 4 and 5 girls, since these are the only outcomes we're interested in.

Simulate The Hypothesis

  1. Define our "null hypothesis" $\Rightarrow$ The probability of a pup being female is $\frac{1}{2}$ (equal chance)
  2. Randomly select $n$ from {"male","female"}, with replacement
  3. Count the number of "female"s
  4. Repeat the process, say 10,000 times
  5. Calculate the proportion of times counts were at least $x$ (p-value)

We get to define our testing hypothesis, here the null, and we could define it based on some other information, like the fact that we currently have 50.5 percent females in the US Population.

The Algorithm

Pseudocode



initializations()

for (i = 1,2,...,10000) {

	selection = randomly select from {0,1} with replacement

	female_count[i] = count(selection == 1)

}

p_value = count(female_count >= 4) / 10000

Python Code



import numpy as np



# Run 10000 simulations

n_sims = 10000

n_mice = 5  # Sample size in original data

p_null = 0.5 # Probability of a pup being female



# Initialize array to store female counts

female_counts = np.zeros(n_sims)

choices = ["male","female"]



# Run simulations

for i in range(n_sims):

	# Randomly select "male" or "female" with replacement

	selection = np.random.choice(choices, size=n_mice, p=[1-p_null, p_null])

	# Count number of females

	female_counts[i] = np.sum(selection == "female")



# Calculate p-value (proportion of simulations with >= 4 females)

p_value = np.mean(female_counts >= 4)

print(f"The probability of 4 or more girls in 5 pups is: p={p_value:.4f}")

What's The Difference?

For discrete events, the binomial hypothesis is a fixed probability calculation if and only if the underlying assumptions are true.

The bootstrap method is a resampling technique that allows us to estimate probabilities by sampling from the observed data. This approach doesn't rely on strict theoretical assumptions, making it more adaptable to real-world complexities where ideal conditions may not be met.

While the binomial approach offers exact probabilities under ideal assumptions, the bootstrap method provides flexibility, allowing for more accurate approximations in practical scenarios. This is mostly what we'll focus on in this course.

So it must be the case that the probability of being a female is exactly 0.5.

For bootstrapping, we don't have to rely on the exact assumption of 0.5 probability.

While the binomial approach offers exact probabilities under ideal assumptions, the bootstrap method provides flexibility, allowing for more accurate approximations in practical scenarios. This distinction underscores the frequentist interpretation of probability, which defines probability as the long-run frequency of an event occurring over repeated trials under the same conditions.
