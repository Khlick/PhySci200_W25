What is Probability?

Probability is the measure of the likelihood that an event will occur. It quantifies uncertainty and is expressed as a number between 0 and 1, where 0 indicates impossibility and 1 indicates certainty.

Terminology

Meaning

Sample Space ($S$):

The set of all possible outcomes of a probabilistic experiment. For example, in a genetic test for a disease mutation, the sample space might include outcomes like 'Carrier' and 'Non-carrier'.

Event:

A subset of the sample space. An event can consist of one or more outcomes. For instance, the event 'Person is a Carrier' includes all outcomes where the genetic test indicates presence of the mutation.

Probability of an Event ($P(A)$):

The likelihood of an event occurring, which is a number between 0 and 1. If $P(A) = 1$, the event is certain to happen, and if $P(A) = 0$, the event is impossible. For example, if the probability of being a carrier for a specific genetic mutation is 0.05, it can be written as $P(\text{Carrier}) = 0.05$.

Approaches

Probability theory deals with the likelihood of different outcomes in various scenarios. There are three primary approaches:

Approach Description
Subjective Based on personal judgment and available information. It is often used in scenarios where hard data is scarce, and decisions are made based on beliefs or expert knowledge.
Classical Assumes that all outcomes in a sample space are equally likely. It is best suited for situations where you can clearly define all possible outcomes, and each outcome has the same chance of occurring, i.e., symmetrical weight.
Empirical Relies on actual data collected from experiments or observations. Empirical probability is calculated by dividing the number of times an event occurs by the total number of trials or observations.

Each approach offers a unique perspective and methodology for determining the likelihood of events.

There are three primary approaches to understanding and calculating probability:

  • Subjective: a doctor might use subjective probability to estimate the likelihood of a patient's recovery based on their experience and the patient's symptoms. Useful when thinking about how to test or experiment something.
  • Classical: An example is tossing a fair coin, where the chances of landing heads or tails are both 50%.
  • Empirical: It is particularly useful in situations where historical data is available, such as calculating the probability of rain based on weather records.

Empirical Probability

Empirical probability is calculated based on observed data or past experiences. It measures the likelihood of an event by dividing the number of observed occurrences by the total number of observations.

Relative frequency, the ratio of an event's occurrences to total trials, forms the empirical basis for calculating an event's probability.

$$ \text{Relative Frequency} = \frac{\text{Frequency of the event occurring}}{\text{Total number of trials}} $$

Example: Consider a biomechanical experiment where a prosthetic knee is subjected to repeated stress tests. Out of 500 tests, the knee joint fails 5 times. The relative frequency of failure is calculated as: $$ \text{Relative Frequency of Failure} = \frac{5}{500} = 0.01 $$ This means that the probability of failure in a single stress test, based on past data, is 1%.

Random Variables

The expectation (or expected value) of a random variable is a measure of the central tendency of its distribution.

For a random variable \( X \) with discrete outcomes \( x_i \) and corresponding probabilities \( P(X = x_i) \), the expectation is given by:

$$E[X] = \sum_{i=1} x_i \cdot P(X = x_i)$$

Consider a random variable with 2 equal bins: Bin $1\rightarrow 0$, Bin $2 \rightarrow 1$.

\( E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 0 + \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2} \)

Now consider a scenario with 7 bins: mapped to 0 through 6 values

\( E[X] = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 2 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 3 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 4 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 5 \cdot \frac{1}{7} + 6 \cdot \frac{1}{7} \)

Simplifying:

\( E[X] = \frac{1}{7} (0 + 1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) = \frac{1}{7} \cdot 21 = 3 \)

The Normal Distribution

The normal (or Gaussian) distribution is a continuous probability distribution characterized by a symmetric, bell-shaped curve.

PDF:

$$f(\class{warning}{x}; \class{info}{\mu}, \class{info}{\sigma}) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^2}$$
  • $\mu$ = Central Tendency ($E[X]$)
  • 68% of the observations lie within 1 standard "distance" of the center
  • $\sigma$ = Spread ($E[X-E[X]]$)
  • 95% lie within 1.96 standard "distance" of the center
  • $x$ = Specific value of the continuous variable
  • 99% lie within 2.58 standard "distance" of the center

Often denoted $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the normal distribution is special as it underscores the Central Limit Theorem's revelation that sums of independent variables universally converge to this form, regardless of their initial distributions.

The normal distribution stands as a fundamental pillar in statistics. We will cover many other continuous distributions as we go along, but we start with the gaussian.

If the area under the curve sums to 1 and represents the sum of all P(X=X), then any point X_i has the probability f(x;mu,sigma).

Quantile Probability Points

Probability points are used to determine the specific quantiles of a distribution for a given dataset of size N. These points help in mapping data to their respective quantiles in a theoretical distribution.

For a dataset of size \(N\), the \(i^{th}\) probability point \(p_i\) is calculated as:

\(p_i = \frac{i - \alpha}{N + 1 - 2\alpha}\),
where \(i\) ranges from 1 to \(N\), and \(\alpha\) is an adjustment factor (commonly 0.5 or \(\frac{3}{8}\) for small samples).

Example for \(N = 10\):

  • Using \(\alpha = 0.5\), calculate \(p_i\) for \(i = 1\) to \(10\).
  • \(p_1 = \frac{1 - 0.5}{10 + 1 - 2 \times 0.5} = \frac{0.5}{10} = 0.05\)
  • Similarly, \(p_{10} = \frac{10 - 0.5}{10 + 1 - 2 \times 0.5} = \frac{9.5}{10} = 0.95\)
  • \(N < 10\): \(\alpha=0.375\)
  • \(10 \leq N < 50\): \(\alpha=0.4\)
  • \(N \geq 50\): \(\alpha = 0.5\)

Now we can map our sample size to the cumulative probability points in the normal distribution. More specifically, to the standard normal with mean 0 and standard deviation of 1.

But, recall I said the standard normal distribution assumes values on the interval -infty to + infty? We have to deal with that.

Probability Points And The ECDF

Empirical Cumulative Density Function (ECDF)

A step function that estimates the cumulative distribution function of a sample by mapping each data point to its corresponding percentile rank within the sample

\(F_n(x) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} I(X_i \le x)\)

The formula for probability points is adjusted from the ECDF's simple \(1/n\) weighting to accommodate the range of the inverse quantile function of a normal distribution.

Specifically, as the inverse quantile function approaches infinity at \(p = 1\), using a midpoint approach \(\left(\frac{i - 0.5}{n}\right)\) prevents the calculation of impossible values and better aligns with the theoretical distribution, providing a more accurate comparison.

Recall the cumulative density function for the normal distribution is the sum of the probability density function. The total area under the curve is 1, the upper limit of the CDF.

Because the CDF (theory) expects values from -infty to +infty, we can't simply map our sample to the CDF. Instead, we use the empirical cumulative density function (ECDF) to estimate the CDF.

If we have probability points that map directly to the sample, we would end up with a cumulative density of 1, which would correspond to the +infinity value, so our probability points formula allows us to adjust for this, making the estimates from the standard normal distribution more accurate.

Assessing Normality

Before using methods that assume a Normal Distribution, we need to assess whether the data are normally distributed.

Calculate probability points for sample quantiles: $$p_i = \frac{i - \alpha}{N + 1 - 2\alpha}$$

Choose \(\alpha\) based on sample size:

  • \(N < 10\): \(\alpha=0.375\) (Blom's adjustment)
  • \(10 \leq N < 50\): \(\alpha=0.4\) (improves mid-range fit)
  • \(N \geq 50\): \(\alpha = 0.5\) (median rank)

The inverse quantile function allows us to calculate the standard normal value:

$$F^{-1}(p) = Z_p \quad \text{where} \quad P(Z \leq Z_p) = p$$

Viewing Normality: QQ plot

  • The sample data are plotted against the standard normal (theoretical) quantiles.
  • The unity line represents perfect normality; sample points should cluster around this line.
  • Points on the unity line suggest that the sample distribution is similar to a normal distribution.
  • Deviation from the unity line implies potential skewness or kurtosis in the sample distribution.
  • The Quantile-Quantile Plot provides a visual comparison between empirical data quantiles and theoretical normal quantiles.

The QQ plot visualizes the sample quantiles against the theoretical quantiles from a standard normal distribution. A linear relationship suggests normality; deviations from this can inform about the distribution's skewness or tail behavior.

Quantile Simulation

Procedure for the Quantile-Quantile simulation:

  1. Sort the data
  2. Determine the probability points
  3. Calculate the normal quantiles and QLine
  4. Initialize arrays for simulations
  5. For each simulation:
    1. Draw a random sample of size N with replacement from the data (sorted)
    2. Fit the Q1 and Q3
    3. Calculate the QLine
  6. Calculate the confidence bands

# Data:  [166.6 400.6 407.9 415.1 445.1 466.1 557.9 664.2 729.7 794.2]

import numpy as np

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

import scipy.stats as stats

# Get data properties

N = len(data)

B = 10000

# Sort the data (ascending)

sorted_data = np.sort(data)

# Determine Probability points

alpha = 0.375 if N < 10 else 0.4 if N < 50 else 0.5

ppoints = (np.arange(N) + alpha(N)) / (N + 1 - 2 * alpha(N))

# Calculate the corresponding normal distribution quantiles

normal_quantiles = stats.norm.ppf(ppoints)

# Actual qline:

qline = np.polyval(

	np.polyfit(

		np.quantile(normal_quantiles, [0.25, 0.75], method='median_unbiased'),

		np.quantile(sorted_data, [0.25, 0.75], method='median_unbiased'),

		1), normal_quantiles

)

# initialize arrays for bootstraps

bootstrap_qlines = np.zeros((N,B))

for i in range(B):

	# Draw a random sample of size N with replacement from the data (sorted)

	bootstrap_sample = np.sort(np.random.choice(data, size=N, replace=True))

	# fit to Q1 and Q3

	bootstrap_qlines[:,i] = np.polyval(

		np.polyfit(

			np.quantile(normal_quantiles, [0.25, 0.75], method='median_unbiased'),

			np.quantile(bootstrap_sample, [0.25, 0.75], method='median_unbiased'),

			1), normal_quantiles

	)

# Calculate confidence bands (2.5th and 97.5th percentiles)

confidence_bands = np.percentile(bootstrap_qlines, [2.5, 97.5], axis=1).T

  • Normal: Points closely follow the unity line, indicating a normal distribution.
  • Left Skew: Points curve below at the lower end and below the line at the upper end, reflecting a longer tail on the left and shorter on the right.
  • Right Skew: Points curve above the line at the lower end and above at the upper end, indicating a longer tail on the right and shorter on the left.

Interpreting QQ Plots

Deviations from the unity line have different implications:

  • Bimodal: Shows a distinctive "S" shaped curve or multiple clusters along the QQ line, indicating multiple peaks in the data distribution.
  • Fat Tails: Points diverge from the unity line at the ends, suggesting excess kurtosis and a higher likelihood of extreme values (i.e., longer tails at both ends).
  • Skinny Tails: Points diverge from the unity line at the ends, indicating a lower likelihood of extreme values (i.e., shorter tails on both sides).

Note that bimodal crosses in the middle and then also has skinny tails here. Where the skinny tails distribution doesn't cross so much (but might have multiple peaks randomly due to construction)

To answer a question about a population (of data).

def bootstrapper(dataset, B=10000, statistic=np.median):

	boot_stats = np.zeros(B)

	for i in range(B):

		boot_data = np.random.choice(dataset, size=70, replace=True)

		boot_stats[i] = statistic(boot_data)

	return boot_stats

# Use the function to create a resampling distribution (theta hat)

bootstraps = bootstrapper(dataset, B=10000, statistic=np.median)

To mimic the replication of the experiment, we resample our sample with replacement.

For each resample, we calculate the statistic (e.g., median).

We repeat this process 10,000 times to create a resampling distribution.

See how our resampling distribution is not quite centered on the true median? This difference is called "bias".

Sampling Distribution

A sampling distribution is the distribution of sample statistics computed for randomly selected samples from the same underlying population.

CLT: For most of the parameters we consider, if samples are randomly selected and the sample size is large enough, the sampling distribution will be generally symmetric and bell-shaped and centered at the value of the population parameter.

Let's look at an example.

Considering Sample Size

Sample Size Matters!
Increasing sample size reduces the variability of sample statistics, making them more accurate (narrower sampling distribution).

Larger sample sizes lead to reduced uncertainty and narrower confidence intervals.

If the sampling distribution in narrower, what also happens to the standard Error? 95% Confidence Interval?

Think about SE and CI calculations. sigma smaller or n larger (both too), then 1.96*se also smaller.

Estimating The Population Parameters

The Sampling Distribution

We only have a limited budget, enough to take a sample size of 6, "What is the value of $\mu$?"

We randomly select 6 students ($n=6$) as a representative sample, calculate $\bar{x}$ and see $\bar{x}\approx\mu$.

If we take another random sample, $\bar{x}$ would be a bit different (but still $\bar{x}\approx\mu$).

Lots of possible different samples from the population, lots of slightly different values for the statistics of interest ($\bar{x}\approx\mu$).
All of these possible statistics of interest define the sampling distribution of the statistic.

Each statistic of interest has its own sampling distribution, the sampling distribution of the mean won't be the same as the one for the median, etc..

Last fragment. Note the hierarchy of the labels and names: greek letters, like mu and sigma, for the population PARAMETERS, lower case letters with bar or sometimes tilde or hat for sample of size N statistic, and finally, "error" standard for normal distributions or confidence interval for the sampling distribution.

So, sigma -> population spread, s -> sample spread, se or ci -> sampling distribution spread
