Hypothesis tests and p-values are most useful when you want to test hypotheses about population parameters.

$1-\alpha$ confidence intervals

Confidence intervals are most useful when you want to estimate population parameters.

Confidence intervals give you a range of plausible values for your parameter.

Note: Usually $\alpha$=0.05.

Sir Ronald Fisher introduced the concept of the 0.05 level as a cut-off point for statistical significance, but he did not intend for it to become as definitive and universally applied as it has become in contemporary research. Fisher suggested that a p-value of 0.05 was a convenient threshold to consider a result sufficiently rare under the null hypothesis, thereby justifying its rejection.

The History of the Alpha Level in Hypothesis Testing

The alpha level of 0.05, a standard threshold for statistical significance, was popularized by Ronald A. Fisher in the 1920s. Originally suggested as a convenient cut-off for rejecting the null hypothesis, it implied a result would only occur by chance about 1 in 20 times under the null hypothesis.

It is convenient to take this point as a limit in judging whether a deviation is to be considered significant or not.

-Sir Ronald Fisher

Despite its arbitrary nature, the 0.05 level became widely adopted due to its balance between Type I and Type II errors. Over time, it has become the conventional standard across various scientific disciplines.

Recent debates challenge this standard, suggesting adjustments or alternative approaches like confidence intervals or Bayesian statistics to better reflect the complexity of scientific evidence.

What is the probability of making a Type I or Type II error?

Probability of Type I Errors

If the null hypothesis is true:

  • 5% of statistics will be in the most extreme 5%
  • 5% of statistics will give p-values less than 0.05
  • 5% of statistics will lead to rejecting H$_0$ at $\alpha=0.05$

If $\alpha=0.05$, there is a 5% chance of a Type I error

If the null hypothesis is true:

  • 1% of statistics will be in the most extreme 1%
  • 1% of statistics will give p-values less than 0.01
  • 1% of statistics will lead to rejecting H$_0$ at $\alpha=0.01$

If $\alpha=0.01$, there is a 1% chance of a Type I error

For each test, the probability of making a Type I error is $\alpha$.

Probability of Type II Errors

How can we reduce the probability of making a Type II Error

That is, how could we increase the probability of rejecting the null when the null hypothesis is rejectable, i.e., false?

1 Increase the sample size

Larger sample sizes decrease the variability of the sampling distribution.

  • With small sample sizes, even large differences or effects may not be statistically significant, and Type II errors are common.
  • But with large sample sizes, even a very small difference or effect can be statistically significant

Increased $n$ reduces chances of Type II errors increases statistical power.

Significance Level and Errors

While we wish to avoid both types of errors, in practice we must accept some trade-off between them:

If we set $\alpha$ too low:

  • It becomes more difficult to reject $H_0$ (conservative).
  • This decreases the probability of a Type I error (false positive).
  • But increases the probability of a Type II error (false negative).

If we set $\alpha$ too high:

  • It becomes easier to reject $H_0$ (liberal).
  • This decreases the probability of a Type II error (false negative).
  • But increases the probability of a Type I error (false positive).

Recall that the CDF for the standard normal distribution is $\Phi(Z) = p$, where $\Phi(Z) = \int_{-\infty}^{Z} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{z^2}{2}} \, dz$. Thus, we find the inverse CDF, $\Phi^{-1}(p) = Z$, to get the Z-Value for our Type I and Type II error rates.

We find our Error Rates relative to their sampling distributions: $H_0:\class{danger}{\alpha}$, $H_a:\class{muted}{\beta}$.

Error rates are related by the $\alpha$ level. With $H_0\& H_a$ on the same data space, $\beta$ is $\Phi_a(\Phi^{-1}_0(\alpha))$.

Note that beta is the integration of the Ha distribution from negative infinity to the z value corresponding to the Null distribution's alpha level.

Explanation for Students

When introducing this concept to students, it would be helpful to clarify:

  • Effect Size Consideration: The noncentral t-distribution considers the effect size you expect between groups. For instance, if you believe the new jet engine is more efficient by a certain margin, this margin (quantified by Cohen's d) influences the noncentrality parameter.
  • Connection to H1: This distribution directly relates to the hypothesis that there is a difference (i.e., H1). Unlike the central t-distribution, which is used under H0 (no difference), the noncentral t-distribution takes the presumed effect into account, making it the distribution of the test statistic under H1.
  • Standardization: The term "standardized" here implies that the effect size has been transformed into a scale that is comparable across different studies or contexts, factoring in the sample size and variance.
  • Usage in Power Calculations: Emphasize that this distribution is crucial for calculating the power of a test, which is the probability of correctly rejecting H0 when H1 is true. The area under the curve of the noncentral t-distribution beyond the critical value of the t-test (determined by the significance level alpha) gives us this probability.

Understanding The Significance Level

The significance level $\alpha$, represents the tolerable probability of making a Type I error.

If the consequences of a Type I error are severe (for example, approving a new drug that is potentially dangerous) we might use a very small $\alpha$ (perhaps even $\alpha=0.005$).

If the consequences of a Type II error are severe (for example, failing to diagnose a treatable disease), we would want to make it easier to reject $\mathrm{H}_0$, so we might use a relatively large $\alpha$.

Key idea

Remember that there is always a trade-off between the two types of errors, so people usually use the common significance levels of 5%, 10% or 1% (but where the perfect balance lies is a complicated question and the increasing subject of debate).

Introduction to Types of Power Analysis

Statistical Power Analysis is a crucial aspect of experimental design, used to determine the likelihood that a study will detect an effect when there is an effect to be detected. It addresses two types of errors:

  • Type I Error ($\alpha$): Incorrectly rejecting the null hypothesis when it is true.
  • Type II Error ($\beta$): Failing to reject the null hypothesis when it is false.

There are two primary types of power analysis:

  • Post Hoc Power Analysis: Conducted after a study to evaluate the likelihood of committing a Type II error, i.e., failing to detect an effect that is present. This analysis is typically used to understand the power limitations of a completed study.
  • A Priori Power Analysis: Conducted before a study begins, it is used to determine the necessary sample size to achieve a desired power level, typically set to 80%, to confidently detect an expected effect.

Understanding and applying these analyses help ensure that engineering studies are designed efficiently, with sufficient power to yield meaningful results.

False Positive Rate ($\alpha$)

In the long run, if we accept $p \approx 0.05$ as a threshold possibility, we make the correct decision 95 times in 100 experiments. We will misclassify 5% of experiments as positive when this is not the case. The type I error rate is the rate of classification of results as positive (different) when in fact there is no difference: the false positive rate. In the context of trust in results, a false positive rate of 5% is generally considered acceptable.

$\alpha$ is the probability of saying there is an effect, where the effect is truly from random chance.

Powerless Statements

... at this point, I've spent a lot of time with the president. And not once have I seen him do something this was mean, or cruel, or wrong. Not once.

-Elon Musk (2025)

We hear this claim all the time: There is no evidence that X is toxic.

This is not the same as saying X is not toxic.

If a Null Hypothesis cannot be rejected, this is not sufficent evidence that there is NO Effect.

We have to be careful about the language we use. Statements like "I've never seen him do anything mean, or cruel, or wrong" are not helpful. They are not helpful because they are not helpful.

Carl Sagan famously said "extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence", and "Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence".

Summary of Two-Group Testing

  • Nonparametric (Mann-Whitney-Wilcoxon) incurs a loss of power.
  • At tiny sample sizes (4-6), rank bootstrap has problems with false positives.
  • At samples \( n \leq 60 \), twobox overfits.
  • Bigbox usually works well and is slightly more powerful than Student.
  • There are always trade-offs between False Positive Rate and Power.
  • There is no "uniformly most powerful test".
