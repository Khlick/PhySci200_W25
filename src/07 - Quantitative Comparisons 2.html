Comparing Two Groups

Last time, we compared two groups by simulating a null hypothesis that either the two groups came from the same population, or that they came from different populations.

If two samples are from the same population, what is the chance of finding at least this difference?

If two samples are from different populations, what is the chance of finding at least this difference in their centers?

The chance here is quantified by a p-value

Null Hypothesis

Same Population

The Null Hypothesis carries the assumption of equality, i.e.,
two groups come from the same underlying population $\rho$.

\(H_0\): hypothesis | assumptions, $H_0 : \tilde{x}_A = \tilde{x}_B \, | \, \rho_A(x) = \rho_B(x)$

implied for all the true statistics: ($\bar{x}_A = \bar{x}_B$, $\mathrm{m}_A=\mathrm{m}_B$, etc..)

Different Population

The Null Hypothesis carries no assumptions of the underlying populations, allowing for two independent underlying populations.

\(H_0\): hypothesis | assumptions, $H_0 : \tilde{x}_A = \tilde{x}_B \, | \, \rho_A(x) \neq \rho_B(x)$

Recall that we stated xbar_1 - xbar_2 = delta0, where delta0 was the difference in the parameters (mu).

What Is Effect Size

Null hypothesis significance testing (NHST) is the dominant statistical approach in biology, although it has many, frequently unappreciated, problems. Most importantly, NHST does not provide us with two crucial pieces of information: (1) the magnitude of an effect of interest, and (2) the precision of the estimate of the magnitude of that effect. ... Combined use of an effect size and its CIs enables one to assess the relationships within data more effectively than the use of p values, regardless of statistical significance.

Effect Size

Effect Size is a measure of the magnitude of the Statistic of Interest between two groups.

The new idea here is to put a confidence interval on the $\Delta$. Similar to the confidence interval on the statistic of interest, we can put a confidence interval on the effect size, e.g., BCa.

  • There is no "Null" hypothesis. You can think of it like the two-box scenario, without recentering.
  • Resample new statistics from each group separately.
  • Calculate the Statistic of Interest (difference, raito, etc.) from the resampled statistics.
  • Repeat the process 10,000 times.
  • Calculate the Confidence Interval on the statistic of interest.

We will refer to "Statistic of Interest" as "Effect Size" from here on.

Cohen's $\delta$

Cohen's d for Gaussian Distributed Data

In addition to quoting the mean difference and confidence interval, Cohen's \( d \) is a commonly used effect size for mean differences:

\( d = \frac{\mu_A - \mu_B}{\sigma} \)

This is closely related to Student's \( t \)-statistic but without dependence on sample size \( n \):

\( t = \sqrt{n} \cdot d \)

By dividing by the spread \( \sigma \), we obtain a standardized measure. The change in mean is measured in terms of how many standard deviations \( \sigma \).

Interpretation

Since Cohen's \( d \) is standardized, Cohen suggested rough guidelines for interpretation:

  • Small effect: \( d < 0.3 \)
  • Medium effect: \( d \approx 0.5 \)
  • Large effect: \( d > 0.8 \)

This measure is particularly relevant in Gaussian statistics because it normalizes the mean difference by the population variability.

Cohen's d for Unequal Variance

Just as Welch extended Student's \( t \)-test to account for groups with different variances, a similar approach is used here.

The standard deviation used is a "pooled" standard deviation:

\( \sigma_p = \sqrt{\frac{(n_A - 1) \sigma_A^2 + (n_B - 1) \sigma_B^2}{n_A + n_B - 2}} \)

This accounts for differing variances in the two groups while maintaining a standardized effect size calculation.

Cohen's d was introduced by Jacob Cohen in 1969 as a way to quantify the size of an effect in a standardized manner. It has since become a widely used measure in the field of psychology and other social sciences for interpreting the practical significance of research findings. Cohen's work emphasized the importance of not just determining statistical significance, but also understanding the magnitude of the observed effects.

Paired Samples

Paired samples (or dependent samples) occur when 2 or more data points are collected from the same observational unit.

Relationship Conditions For Paired Data

  • There is one pair of response values for each observational unit
  • There is a built-in comparison, each data point in one sample is linked to another data point in the other samples. E.g., Measuring machine performance every month.

Origin Of The Pairing

  • Matching similar individuals to create groups of two: paired design using matching.
  • Measuring the same individual (observational unit) in each condition: paired design using repeated measures.
When a paired design is possible, you typically get more informative results because units within a pair tend to be similar to each other, so differences within such each pair on the response variable tend to be due mainly to the explanatory variable.

Paired Samples

Types and Conditions

Pre-test/post-test samples in which a factor is measured before and after an intervention.

Cross-over trials in which individuals are randomized to two treatments and then the same individuals are crossed-over to the alternative treatment.

Matched samples, in which subjects are matched on inherent characteristics (age, mfr. lot#, etc.).

Duplicate measurements on the same subject.

Any circumstance in which each data point in one sample is uniquely matched to a data point in the second sample.

Paired-Sample Analysis

The key to analyzing paired data is to work with the difference within each pair of data values rather than between the two original samples.
This helps us to eliminate the variability across different units and instead focus on what we really care about: the difference between the maximum frictional forces across formulas.
